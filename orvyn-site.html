Technology

From muscle signal to finger movement

Three core systems work in parallel — sensing your intent, interpreting it, and acting on it — all within 20 milliseconds.

01
sEMG Signal Capture

Surface electrodes placed on the forearm detect microvolt-level electrical signals from 6 muscle groups. Even a partial, stroke-weakened contraction is detected. The signal is amplified, band-pass filtered (20–450 Hz), and streamed to the Teensy 4.0 at ≥1 kHz.

02
TinyML Gesture Classification

The Teensy 4.0 (600 MHz ARM Cortex-M7) extracts features — RMS, MAV, Zero Crossings, Waveform Length — from a 100–200 ms sliding window. A trained SVM classifier (≥90% accuracy target) identifies the intended gesture: rest, open hand, fist, pinch, or individual finger movement.

03
Tendon-Cable Actuation

Five servo motors pull Kevlar tendons (0.8mm, rated 441N) routed through 3D-printed phalange rings. Each finger has 3 DOF: independent extension cable (T1) and paired flexion cables (B1, B2, B3) on either side, enabling both bending and full extension without restricting grip sensation.

Engineering Journey

From sketch to system

Every design decision was driven by a single constraint: what works best for a stroke patient using this at home. Here's how ORVYN evolved.

Phase 1 — Concept
Linkage vs. Cable: Choosing the actuation path

The first question wasn't hardware — it was mechanism. Rigid linkage systems, common in research exoskeletons, offer deterministic motion but require precise length calibration per hand size. They're inflexible across users. We moved to a tendon-cable system: Kevlar cables anchored to 3D-printed phalange rings, routed through dorsal guides, all pulled by servo motors in the main forearm unit. The anchors are adjustable, so the same glove fits different hand sizes.

Cable: Kevlar 0.8mm Ø Force per finger: 3–6N Phalange rings: 14 total DOF: 3 per finger, 2 thumb
Phase 2 — Sketching
Finding where the main unit should live

Early concepts placed motors on the dorsal hand for maximum torque efficiency — but a hand weakened by stroke can't support a heavy electronics housing. Placing it on the back or shoulder required longer cables and more powerful (heavier) motors. The compromise: forearm-mounted, secured with Velcro straps. Close enough for efficient force transmission, light enough to wear for rehabilitation sessions.

Figure 2.1.4 — Initial design sketches exploring main unit placement and EMG sensor positioning across front and back of forearm
Phase 3 — 3D Visualization
Full system render with annotated components

The final mechanical concept was rendered in CAD to verify cable routing before printing. The render confirmed the three-pathway cable design for each finger: T1 (extension) runs along the dorsal top; B1/B2/B3 (flexion) run along the sides — keeping the palm clear of cables so patients can feel objects they grip. Cable guides at each phalange joint constrain the path without friction spikes.

Figure 2.1.5 — Final 3D concept render: sEMG sensor placement on inner forearm, force transmission routing, wrist cable guides, and dual extension/flexion cable paths per finger
Phase 4 — Electronics
From single ESP32 to dual-MCU architecture

The first prototype ran everything on one ESP32. During testing: the ADC noise polluted sEMG readings, WiFi RF activity interfered with analog accuracy, and the processor couldn't maintain consistent PWM timing while handling EMG classification. The solution was a clean separation — Teensy 4.0 handles all real-time processing and motor control, while an ESP32-WROOM handles all wireless communication. UART links the two. The result: clean EMG, jitter-free PWM, and live WiFi dashboard without any cross-contamination.

Teensy 4.0: 600MHz ARM Cortex-M7 ESP32-WROOM: WiFi 2.4GHz + BLE UART @ 115200 baud PCA9685: 16-ch PWM servo driver XL4015 Dual Buck Converters
Phase 5 — Software
From test loops to modular TinyML pipeline

Code started as simple while-loops: read EMG → threshold check → open/close. Iterative testing revealed the need for proper windowed feature extraction, confidence-gated gesture classification, and slew-rate-limited servo commands. The final pipeline is three clean modules: SAP (Signal Acquisition & Processing), IRM (Intent Recognition Module using SVM), and MCA (Motion Control & Actuation). Python scripts for ML training auto-export SVM weights as C++ arrays for Teensy deployment.

Features: RMS, MAV, ZC, WL Window: 100–200ms sliding Classifier: SVM (TinyML, <50KB) Gestures: 6 classes + rest Update: 40–50 predictions/sec

The Problem
Paralysis of Intent
After a stroke, patients retain the desire to move their hand — but the neural pathway is damaged. Their muscles receive a partial signal but cannot follow through, degrading quality of life.
The Solution
ORVYN Completes the Movement
ORVYN's sEMG sensors detect the residual electrical signal. Its proportional control system translates that intent — however weak — into full assisted finger movement via 4 servo motors and Kevlar tendons.
